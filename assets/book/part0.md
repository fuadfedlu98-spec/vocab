# PART 0 — INITIAL FLUENCY ASSESSMENT

You are about to find out, with numbers instead of guesses, exactly where your English actually stands. Not where fifteen years of classes suggest it should stand. Where it stands right now, today, when you open your mouth and something has to come out in real time.

This will feel uncomfortable in places. Good. That discomfort is data. A student who reads engineering papers for breakfast and then freezes for three seconds trying to say "I disagree with that assumption" out loud is not weak — he's just never measured the specific gap between what he understands and what he can produce. This assessment measures it precisely, in ten dimensions, so that sixty days from now you can run the exact same test and see, in hard numbers, what changed.

You will not repeat this test until Day 60. Not Day 30, not "whenever I feel ready." The comparison only works if nothing about the conditions changes between the two measurements — same prompts, same rubric, same room, same pressure. Resist the urge to check your progress early. You'll know it's working from the drills themselves; save the scoreboard for the day it's meant to be read.

### Before You Start

You need three things: a phone or laptop that can record audio, a quiet room, and a notebook or the practice log in Appendix E. Nothing else. No app to install, no test to schedule, no teacher to book.

Set aside 90–120 minutes. You don't have to do it in one sitting — three sessions of 30–40 minutes across one day works fine, and for some sections (listening, reading) a fresh mind matters more than finishing fast. What matters is finishing all ten sections before you touch Chapter 1. Chapter 1 assumes this baseline exists.

Score honestly. Nobody sees this except you. A generous score today just makes Day 60 look less impressive than it actually was.

**Scoring scale used throughout Part 0:** every dimension is scored 0–4. This is a separate scale from the 1–5 drill difficulty rating introduced in Chapter 1 — don't confuse the two. Here, 0 means the skill is essentially absent under pressure, 4 means it holds up at native-speaker level under the same pressure. Most engineering students with your background will score low on some dimensions and surprisingly high on others — that unevenness is exactly the picture this test is designed to produce.

## 1. Speaking Fluency

**Purpose:** Fluency is measured here as flow, not correctness — how continuously you can produce spoken English without stalling, restarting, or defaulting to silence while you search for a word.

**Instructions:** Set a timer for 90 seconds. Record yourself speaking, without preparation, on this prompt: *"Describe a technical problem you solved recently, and explain how you solved it."* Speak until the timer ends, even through pauses. Do not stop the recording to think. Do not restart if you make a mistake — a real conversation doesn't give you a restart button either.

Afterward, replay the recording once and count: total pauses longer than 2 seconds, and total self-corrections (times you started a sentence, stopped, and rebuilt it).

**Scoring Rubric:**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | Frequent long silences (5+ seconds); speech breaks down before 30 seconds of content |
| 1 | Speaks in short bursts of 3–5 words separated by long pauses; heavy reliance on filler ("um," "how do I say") |
| 2 | Maintains speech with noticeable pausing every sentence or two; ideas are choppy but understandable |
| 3 | Mostly continuous speech with occasional pausing at word-retrieval points; recovers smoothly |
| 4 | Continuous, natural flow; pauses are placed like a native speaker's, for breath and emphasis, not word search |

**Interpretation:** A score of 0–1 here, paired with strong reading comprehension, confirms this book's core diagnosis: the bottleneck is production, not knowledge. That's expected, not alarming — it's the exact gap Part I is built to close.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, expect a full rubric point of improvement at minimum on this same prompt category (a different specific prompt, same structure). Pause count under 90 seconds should roughly halve.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 10 minutes, including replay and scoring.

## 2. Pronunciation

**Purpose:** To identify which specific sounds and patterns — not "your accent" in general — are creating actual intelligibility problems, so Chapters 6–7 can target them instead of working on pronunciation in the abstract.

**Instructions:** Record yourself reading this paragraph aloud, at normal conversational speed, not slowly and carefully:

*"Right now, I think the best approach is to run three tests, compare the results, and then decide which model actually fits the data. If the third test fails, we'll have to revisit our assumptions about the load."*

Then record yourself saying these ten word pairs, back to back: ship/sheep, think/sink, very/berry, light/right, world/word, thirty/dirty, vowel/bowel, worked/walked, live/leave, cheap/chip.

**Scoring Rubric:**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | A listener unfamiliar with your L1 would frequently lose the meaning of full sentences |
| 1 | Understandable with effort; several sounds are consistently substituted or dropped |
| 2 | Generally understandable; 2–4 specific sound patterns cause repeated confusion |
| 3 | Clearly understandable; a noticeable accent remains but rarely blocks meaning |
| 4 | Intelligibility is not a factor; accent is present but has no impact on comprehension |

**Interpretation:** The word-pair recording matters more than the paragraph. Listen for which pairs you pronounced identically — those are your priority sounds, not a generic "work on pronunciation" plan.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, the specific sound pairs flagged here should be audibly distinct on replay. Full "accent removal" is not the target and was never the point — see Chapter 7.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 10 minutes.

## 3. Listening

**Purpose:** To separate two different comprehension levels — clear, moderate-speed audio versus fast, natural native conversation — because your fifteen years of subtitle-supported input almost certainly built the first without building the second.

**Instructions:** Find two YouTube clips: one clear interview or explainer video (a TED talk works), and one clip of two native speakers talking casually to each other — a podcast with natural back-and-forth, not a scripted show. Watch each for two minutes with no subtitles. Immediately after each clip, without rewinding, write down everything you understood in as much detail as possible.

**Scoring Rubric (score each clip separately):**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | Caught isolated words only; no coherent thread of meaning |
| 1 | Followed the general topic but missed most specific content |
| 2 | Followed the main points with gaps in detail |
| 3 | Followed nearly everything; missed occasional fast or idiomatic phrases |
| 4 | Followed everything, including tone and implication |

**Interpretation:** A gap of two or more points between the clear-audio score and the natural-conversation score is common and expected — it's the exact gap Chapters 14–15 are designed to close. A gap that doesn't close by Day 60 is the one to flag for extra shadowing work (Chapter 12) rather than more passive listening.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, the natural-conversation score should have closed at least one full point relative to the clear-audio score.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 15 minutes.

---

## 4. Vocabulary

**Purpose:** To measure the real distance between what you recognize and what you can produce on demand — the passive/active gap defined in Chapter 3 — using forced spoken recall instead of a multiple-choice test that would only measure recognition.

**Instructions:** Set a 60-second timer. Speak out loud, in English, as many words as you can that relate to "problems with a piece of software" — not a list of nouns, full attempts at description if a single word won't come ("the thing when it stops responding" counts, but note it as a near-miss). Record this. Then do the same for 60 seconds on "describing someone's personality." Afterward, count actual words/chunks produced correctly, and separately count near-misses where you knew the concept but not the word.

**Scoring Rubric:**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | Fewer than 5 correct items per topic |
| 1 | 5–9 items, frequent near-misses |
| 2 | 10–14 items, occasional near-misses |
| 3 | 15–19 items, fluent retrieval |
| 4 | 20+ items, no noticeable retrieval effort |

**Interpretation:** A high near-miss count is the most useful number here — it's direct evidence of vocabulary sitting in passive storage, unreachable under time pressure. That's precisely what Chapters 16–18 convert.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, expect the correct-item count to rise by at least 40%, and the near-miss count to drop sharply as those specific words move from passive to active.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 8 minutes.

---

## 5. Grammar

**Purpose:** Not to test whether you know grammar rules — your university coursework already proved that in writing. To test whether that knowledge holds up when you don't have time to think about it, which is the only condition that matters for speaking.

**Instructions:** Record yourself answering these five questions immediately, without pausing to plan the grammar first — answer as fast as you'd answer a colleague:
1. "What would you have done differently on your last project?"
2. "How long have you been studying English?"
3. "What's something you used to believe that turned out to be wrong?"
4. "If the test fails tomorrow, what's your plan?"
5. "Tell me about a mistake someone else made that affected your work."

These target five structures that consistently break down under pressure even for advanced learners: third conditional, present perfect, past habitual, first conditional, and reported/indirect description.

**Scoring Rubric:**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | Structures collapse into simple present tense regardless of what's needed |
| 1 | Attempts the correct structure but with frequent errors that change meaning |
| 2 | Gets the structure roughly right; errors are present but meaning survives |
| 3 | Mostly accurate under pressure; occasional slip |
| 4 | Accurate and unhesitating, matching your known written-grammar level |

**Interpretation:** Most students in your position score noticeably lower here than on a written grammar test covering the same structures. That gap — not a lack of grammar knowledge — is the actual target. This book does not re-teach grammar rules; it trains grammar-under-pressure specifically.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, spoken accuracy on these five structures should approach your written accuracy on the same structures.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 10 minutes.

## 6. Reading

**Purpose:** This dimension is expected to already be strong. Measuring it precisely establishes the asymmetry the entire book is built around — proof, in your own numbers, that the problem was never comprehension.

**Instructions:** Time yourself reading a two-page technical article or paper excerpt in your field (something you haven't read before) at a natural pace. Then, without looking back at the text, summarize it out loud in English for 60 seconds.

**Scoring Rubric:**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0–1 | Reading speed or comprehension is a genuine limiting factor |
| 2 | Solid comprehension, average reading speed for a non-native technical reader |
| 3 | Strong comprehension, efficient reading speed |
| 4 | Comprehension and speed are effectively native-level for technical material |

**Interpretation:** If this scores a 3 or 4 while Speaking Fluency scored a 0 or 1, that gap is the whole book in one comparison. Write both numbers side by side in your log — that's the picture to remember on hard days.

**Improvement Targets:** This dimension isn't a primary training target. A stable or slightly improved score by Day 60 (from reading-to-speak transfer work in Chapter 31) is expected; a drop would be the only concerning result.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 10 minutes.

---

## 7. Writing

**Purpose:** Included only because it's a fast, honest window into structural clarity under time pressure — the same clarity that spoken presentations and technical explanations depend on.

**Instructions:** Set a 10-minute timer. Write a short email to a manager explaining a delay on a task, including a reason and a revised timeline. Don't edit after finishing — the first version is the data point.

**Scoring Rubric:**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0–1 | Ideas are present but structure or grammar errors obscure meaning |
| 2 | Understandable, moderate error rate, adequate structure |
| 3 | Clear, well-structured, minor errors only |
| 4 | Clear, professional, native-level structure and register |

**Interpretation:** Compare this to your Speaking Fluency score. A strong writing score with a weak speaking score confirms, again, that this is a production-speed problem, not a knowledge problem — writing gives you time to plan; speaking doesn't.

**Improvement Targets:** A modest gain here is expected as a side effect of Chapter 30, but this is not a chapter the book spends much time on — don't expect dramatic movement, and don't worry if there isn't much.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 12 minutes.

---

## 8. Automatic Recall Speed

**Purpose:** This is the book's core bottleneck metric — the number every other exercise ultimately exists to move. It measures how fast known words and chunks can be retrieved under a timer, which is a different skill from knowing them at all.

**Instructions:** Have someone read you this list of 20 concepts one at a time (or record yourself reading them with 4-second gaps, then play it back and respond in the gaps): deadline, disagree, root cause, in the meantime, trade-off, bottleneck, as far as I know, follow up, workaround, assumption, edge case, on track, bandwidth, prioritize, in other words, dependency, from scratch, rule of thumb, scope creep, catch up. For each one, say the English word or chunk out loud as fast as you can once you hear the concept named in your native language (have a translation list ready, or use the English word as a prompt and say a one-sentence usage instead).

Time each response from the prompt ending to your voice starting.

**Scoring Rubric (average response time across 20 items):**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | Average over 4 seconds; frequent complete blocks |
| 1 | Average 3–4 seconds |
| 2 | Average 2–3 seconds |
| 3 | Average 1–2 seconds |
| 4 | Under 1 second — approaching automatic, native-like retrieval |

**Interpretation:** This number, more than any other in Part 0, is the one to watch across the whole book. Automaticity — instant, non-deliberate retrieval — is what all the spacing and retrieval-practice drills from Chapter 2 onward are built to produce.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, average response time should drop by at least 40%, with a similar 20-item test using new vocabulary from the book's active-vocabulary lists.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 10 minutes.

## 9. Thinking in English

**Purpose:** To catch, honestly, whether you're mentally translating before speaking — the single biggest hidden speed cost for advanced comprehenders, and often invisible to the person doing it.

**Instructions:** Record yourself narrating your immediate surroundings out loud in English for 60 seconds — describe the room, what you can see, what you're doing right now. While recording, pay attention to whether a native-language sentence forms in your head before the English one comes out, or whether English arrives directly. Immediately after, write one honest sentence about which one happened, and where in the recording you noticed a translation-shaped pause.

**Scoring Rubric:**
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | Constant, noticeable translation; visible delay before nearly every sentence |
| 1 | Frequent translation, especially for anything slightly complex |
| 2 | Translation on complex ideas only; simple description flows directly |
| 3 | Occasional translation under pressure or novelty; mostly direct |
| 4 | No noticeable translation step; English forms directly |

**Interpretation:** This is a self-report cross-checked against the recording's pause pattern, not a perfectly objective measure — but the pauses in the recording rarely lie, even when the self-report is generous. Compare the two.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, translation-shaped pauses should be rare and limited to genuinely novel or complex ideas, not routine description. This is the direct target of Chapter 5.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 8 minutes.

---

## 10. Confidence

**Purpose:** A self-report instrument — useful, but only when checked against the evidence sitting in the other nine recordings rather than trusted on its own, since confidence and actual ability are often out of sync in both directions.

**Instructions:** Answer honestly, on a 0–4 scale, each of these:
- How comfortable would you be joining an unplanned English conversation right now?
- How comfortable would you be answering an unexpected technical question in English in front of colleagues?
- How much do you avoid speaking opportunities because of anticipated difficulty, rather than actual difficulty?
(Reverse-score the third question: high avoidance = low score.)

**Scoring Rubric:** Average the three scores directly; no separate rubric needed beyond the 0–4 scale above.

**Interpretation:** Look for mismatch. A student who scores a 3 on Speaking Fluency but a 1 on Confidence is dealing with a perception problem as much as a skill problem — worth naming honestly now, because it changes how Day 60's results should be read. A confidence score that rises faster than the actual skill scores is also worth noticing; it's not a failure, but it's a reason to keep trusting the recordings over the feeling.

**Improvement Targets:** By Day 60, expect Confidence to track — not wildly outpace or lag — the average movement across the other nine dimensions. If it diverges sharply, Chapter 38 (plateaus) is the place to dig into why.

**Estimated Completion Time:** 5 minutes.
